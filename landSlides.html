Introduction

Rock and sedimentary cuts collapse on mountain roads and higher from time to time. This is due to several factors, including seismic activity, human activity, groundwater pressure, and geological factors, which can move large rocks and accumulated sediments and thus fall on roads and residential complexes, causing many damages that constitute a real danger. There are many studies that have been conducted to evaluate the causes of rockfall and the extent of its impact (McCauely et. al., 1985). There are some studies that were conducted in many regions and most of them were found to be due to 14 factors. Often most of the collapsed materials can be trapped in the protection trench below the rock cut, but sometimes these collapsed materials can exceed the protection trench to reach the road and cause several damage to the roads. In addition, human injuries or deaths may occur as a result of these avalanches. What increases the risk is the design and implementation of mountain roads without taking into account the presence of a protection trench beneath the rock cut. Highways that pass through mountain slopes or obstacles are not devoid of collapses, so they often require careful studies in addition to measurements that are used in operations to control the phenomenon of mountain collapses (rock falls - sedimentary/rock slides - and sedimentary flow). And to maintain the safety of drivers. Cars require that the rock blocks on the roads be safe. The horrific fall of rocks can cause property damage and unexpected human losses, especially in crowded areas. Ensuring the stability of rock masses on mountain roads requires an evaluation of the rock structures. Usually, rockfall occurs due to the occurrence of previous rock separations, so it is necessary to recognize and identify them (Piteau 1979a, b, c, d, e). The phenomenon of mountain collapses is a natural phenomenon that depends on many geological, climatic and human-induced factors Wyllie2018. which is next:

  1. The presence of weak zones and faults.
  2. Joints in rocks, especially if their angles of inclination are high and towards the road or important areas.
  3. Urban expansion and the subsequent change in the levels of some areas, in addition to the construction of longitudinal and transverse roads, which led to a change in the mountainous features.
  4. Rain and water and their effect on penetration into rocks and sediments, which leads to weakening the strength of rocks and sediments.
  5. The nature of rocks and the weathering processes that lead to the weakening of rocks.
  6. Water seepage from human activities in mountainous areas.
  7. Lack of good drainage of rainwater from mountainous areas.

Risks of Landslides

Landslides are considered a sudden geological hazard, and the risk of movements of ground materials depends on the slope of the slopes, the degree of water saturation, and the geological and engineering properties of the rocks and soil. Obstacles and roads that penetrate mountainous areas are considered the areas most vulnerable to mountain avalanches in the Kingdom. The types of mountain collapses are divided into several types:

  • Rapid fall of rocks from the top of high and steep slopes.
  • The sliding of rocks on geological structures, including faults, joints, and shear zones. This process occurs at different speeds, some at one level, some at two levels, some circular, and some overturning.
  • Flow of sedimentary fragments (soil and rocks), where sedimentary flows occur at different speeds in the presence of water.
  • Rock crawling occurs in specific types of rocks and in a very slow movement that cannot be followed with the naked eye.

Learning Objectives

  • Study the geometric properties of mountain slopes and perform digital modeling for each slope, in order to develop the necessary solutions and recommendations to reduce and mitigate the risks of mountain slides.

Working Proccess

Field work is conducted through field visits, first to divide and number road stations, number unstable areas, and conduct field studies to identify potential danger areas, taking into account the most influential factors. Stations have been identified on the road, and the coordinates of these stations and unstable areas are taken. Field measurements are carried out using a compass, a geological hammer, a dimensional device, and collecting samples of rocks and sediments, in addition to field studies to identify the degrees of weathering, the most important separations, areas of weakness, separations, etc. In addition, a study was carried out of the impact of seismic activity and industrial explosions on the region by carrying out some seismic measurements on site from While using some devices, drones are used to study the site and take pictures with an accuracy of 10 cm and maps of elevations with an accuracy of approximately 10 cm.
