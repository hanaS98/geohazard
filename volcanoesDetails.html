Introduction

Volcanoes are cracks and weak points in the Earth's crust from which magma (molten rocks), gases and vapors present in the Earth's interior emerge to its surface, and the rise of these materials is sometimes accompanied by a severe explosion.

Volcano parts:

The volcano consists of three main parts:

  1. Cone: This is the shape that makes up the volcano’s body, and it is composed of the magma that came out of the volcano’s crater and collected on its sides.
  2. Crater: It is formed after the eruption subsides and takes the shape of an arc at the top of the volcano (the cone). Below the crater is the central opening of the volcano from which most of the magma erupts and emerges. The volcano may have more than one secondary crater in addition to the main one.
  3. The reed: It is also called the channel or neck, through which the magma that emerges from the magma chamber in the ground of the Earth rises to the central vent on the surface. The volcano, next to the main reed, may have more than one secondary reed connected to the secondary craters.

Types of volcanoes:

Volcanoes can be divided according to their shape and the nature of the materials that make up them into:

1-Shield volcanoes (plateau)

It is formed when low-viscosity lava flows quietly from several vents and accumulates on top of each other in the form of layers of lava, spreading over wide areas, and forming a mountain in the form of a wide, low dome with a gentle convexity resembling plateaus. This type of volcano often has many craters, such as a Hazm volcano. Green in Harrat Khaybar and Mauna Loa volcano in Hawaii.

2-Debris volcanoes

The cones of these volcanoes are characterized by their height and steep slope, as the degree of slope is between 30-40 degrees, as a result of the intensity of the explosion, represented by Mount Al-Qadr in Harrat Khaybar.

3-Compound volcanoes

It is an intermediate type between the previous two types and consists of a mixture of lava flows and tephra explosions, as it accumulates in successive layers around the volcano’s opening, forming a conical mountain tower. Examples include Mount Vesuvius and Mount Etna in Italy, and Mount St. Helens volcano in Washington, USA.


causes of volcanoes:

There are two main reasons for magma to rise to the surface of the Earth and flow into lava:

  • The movement of the Earth’s crust, where heat is generated at the junction of two crustal plates, which melts materials at depth, and pressure forces them to seep through the cracks that form an outlet that allows them to emerge to the Earth’s surface.
  • Increased pressure in wide sedimentary basins as a result of sediment accumulation. This pressure pushes the magma of the outer layer of the Earth's mantle through cracks in the crust and emerges in the form of volcanic lava

Indicators indicating the possibility of volcanoes occurring:

Sometimes most volcanic eruptions can be predicted, so scientists have established observatories on the slopes of volcanoes, and equipped them with early warning devices that measure some of the phenomena that usually precede a volcanic eruption, such as:

  1. The ground expands and cracks in the area where the volcano occurred due to the accumulation of magma in the magma chamber.
  2. Earthquakes occur when magma rises from the chamber.
  3. An increase in the temperature of areas adjacent to the volcano, such as lakes and wells
  4. Change in the strength and directions of the Earth’s magnetic fields.
  5. Stressful behavior in some types of animals.
  6. Gas clouds released from the volcano’s vent.
  7. Hearing crackling sounds in the ground as the cloud rises to the top.

6-Volcanic dangers:

The explosions of some volcanoes represent a destructive force equivalent to the explosion of nuclear bombs, such as the St. Helens volcano in Washington state. The volcanic danger (Figure 1) is as follows:

  • Gases that separate from magma during the explosion and mix with the atmosphere and air, causing suffocation and death.
  • Volcanic dust that rises several kilometers above the Earth’s surface and works to block sunlight and change the weather and also causes suffocation due to acid rain.
  • Volcanic dust hinders air traffic in the eruption area.
  • Pyroclastic flows with mud flows that cover tens of kilometers around the volcano and bury the houses and villages surrounding the volcano.
  • Magma or volcanic magma flows cause fires in homes and villages near the volcano.

7-Benefits of volcanoes:

Just as volcanoes have risks, they also have benefits, including:

  • Creating high mountains that may reach thousands of metres, and forming vast plateaus and lakes in the cavities of extinct volcanoes.
  • Using hot spring water resulting from volcanic activity to treat skin diseases because it is rich in minerals.
  • Using lava rocks to pave roads.
  • Use of volcanic ash in the manufacture of pozzolan cement that is resistant to salinity and water.
  • Using the gases it emits as a source of energy and operating electrical generators, such as in Iceland and Italy.
  • Production of chemicals from sulfur deposits resulting from volcanic eruptions.
  • Improving soil fertility.
  • Helping scientists study the Earth’s interior through materials ejected by volcanic eruptions.
